Methodology
This map visualizes signal convergence — the overlap of multiple independent indicators that a county is being targeted for ICE detention facility expansion. A single signal means little; multiple signal types in the same county indicate a pattern.

How the score works

Each county receives a heat score based on the number and type of signals detected. Signals are weighted by predictive value: an existing IGSA scores higher than a real estate listing. Each signal type is capped to prevent one category from dominating.

A convergence bonus rewards diversity: +10 points per signal type beyond 2, and +15 more if 5 or more types are present. Three different signal types in one county is far more predictive than ten entries of the same type.

The thesis

Detention consultants like Sabot Consulting target counties with budget distress, cooperative sheriffs, and existing infrastructure. The pitch follows a playbook: sheriff conference recruitment, closed-session presentations, NDA-protected negotiations, then a commission vote framed as fiscal salvation. By the time the public knows, the deal is nearly done.

This map aims to surface the signals early — when multiple independent indicators converge on the same county, that county is likely being pitched. Early detection enables democratic response.

Limitations

This is an early-warning system, not a confirmed list. A high score means multiple independent signals converge — it does not mean a detention facility deal is confirmed or inevitable. False positives are expected. Some counties with active pipeline work may score low if their signals haven't been captured yet.

287(g) FIPS resolution covers ~79% of agencies. Municipal police departments (as opposed to county sheriffs) often can't be reliably mapped to counties without geocoding.
