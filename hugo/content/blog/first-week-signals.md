The site launched with 13 documented county fights. Within hours, the heatmap had guided us to 20. By the end of the first day, visitor traffic pointed us to stories nobody had reported — and we now have 83 fights documented across 38 states.

Here's how it worked. The heatmap scores every county in America based on detention contracts, 287(g) agreements, and budget distress signals. Those scores told us where to look first. But then something unexpected happened: people started visiting the site, and their browsing patterns told us where to look next. Someone spent three minutes on Crow Wing County, Minnesota. Someone else read every word on four southern Oregon counties. A visitor in the DC area studied Arlington, Virginia's surveillance infrastructure.

Every one of those signals led to a significant finding. People are telling us where to research just by what they look at. There is a lot of latent knowledge out there.

## Minnesota: The Operation Nobody Saw Coming

Washington County, Minnesota scored 31 on the heatmap. Modest. Three IGSA facilities and a county fight. But a visitor spent 46 seconds on the page and clicked through to a specific facility — the Oak Park Heights correctional center.

They weren't looking at the prison. They were looking at the **Woodbury warehouse** in the same county — a newly built, vacant building at 11435 Hudson Road that the Washington Post identified in December 2025 as a planned 1,500-bed ICE processing site. Two hundred people packed the county board meeting on January 13. The board unanimously opposed it. The owner confirmed the next day: no sale.

That was one of three Minnesota facility proposals — all blocked. In Shakopee, developer Opus pulled out after state Rep. Brad Tabke mobilized opposition. In Appleton, CoreCivic's shuttered 1,600-bed Prairie Correctional Facility was named in internal ICE documents as a reactivation target. Faith leaders protested. No contract has been signed.

Then came **Operation Metro Surge**: 2,000 federal agents deployed to the Twin Cities on January 6, 2026. Two U.S. citizens were killed by federal agents — Renee Good on January 7, Alex Pretti on January 24. An estimated 50,000 to 75,000 people marched through minus-20-degree weather on January 23. AG Keith Ellison filed a federal lawsuit. The ACLU won an injunction against Freeborn County's 287(g) agreement, establishing that sheriffs cannot sign these unilaterally — the same legal theory now being tested in Pinal County, Arizona.

**Update, April 13:** Visitor traffic revealed a second Minnesota story. Someone spent three full minutes on Crow Wing County's page. Someone else studied Hennepin, Freeborn, Kandiyohi, Nobles, St. Louis, and Roseau counties — systematically reading every one. What they knew and we hadn't documented: ICE enforcement has spread from the Twin Cities metro into a network of rural county jails.

Kandiyohi County has quietly become Minnesota's largest ICE detention site — 150 of 190 beds dedicated to ICE under a 22-year contract. Crow Wing County's sheriff signed dual 287(g) agreements (both Task Force and Warrant Service Officer models) without county board approval. In Worthington (Nobles County), the American Prospect documented how fear alone — without a single raid at the JBS meatpacking plant — devastated the local economy. One in three residents is an immigrant.

And the detainees are disappearing from the state entirely. ICE transfers them to Nebraska and Iowa, hundreds of miles from the attorneys and immigration courts that could hear their cases. The three-front legal war — AG opinion, ACLU injunction, and HF 3060 legislation — is Minnesota's attempt to close the barn door. Whether eight county sheriffs comply is another question.

## Colorado: Nine Hold Rooms Nobody Knew About

Someone browsed ten Colorado counties in one session, spending nearly three minutes on Adams County. The heatmap showed a score of 66 with five IGSA facilities. What it couldn't show was the **shadow detention network** underneath.

Colorado Times Recorder revealed in March 2026 that ICE operates nine hold rooms across the state — unofficial detention sites in office buildings, strip malls, and bank buildings. No external signage. 2,831 people held from January through October 2025, ages 1 to 91. One operates from the Pueblo Bank & Trust building at 415 East Pikes Peak Avenue in Colorado Springs. Another from a strip mall in Glenwood Springs. The busiest runs from the ICE field office in Centennial, Arapahoe County — the same county where we found a "Detention Medical Center Expansion Tour" on the March 17 county commission agenda via Legistar.

The formal system: Aurora ICE Processing Center, 1,530 beds, GEO Group, $70 million expansion. Malnutrition documented at 198 calories per lunch against ICE's own 500-calorie minimum. The Supreme Court ruled unanimously against GEO in a forced labor case. Aurora City Council voted 6-4 condemning "ICE overreach." Adams County opened an investigation.

Three new facilities planned would triple Colorado's formal detention capacity: Big Horn in Hudson (1,256 beds, GEO Group, contract signed), Walsenburg (752 beds, CoreCivic), and Ignacio (Southern Ute tribal facility). The ACLU obtained the expansion documents through a public records lawsuit.

**Update, April 13:** Visitors kept coming back to Adams and Arapahoe counties — spending nearly two minutes on Adams, a minute and a half on Arapahoe. The reason became clear: the busiest hold room, DENHOLD, sits in a Centennial office building owned by a Virginia investment firm collecting $2.2 million a year in federal rent. Nearly 1,400 people were processed through it in ten months. No beds. Children as young as one year old. The 72-hour limit was routinely violated — one man was held 39 days.

And when Adams County tried to exercise oversight — sending health officials to investigate illness outbreaks at the Aurora facility — GEO Group blocked them. Staff interviews refused, testing kits never returned. A county government tried and failed to inspect a federal facility within its own borders.

ICE retaliated against Adams County for complying with Colorado's sanctuary law by revoking body writs — refusing to transport detainees to court appearances. It's a deliberate strategy to degrade criminal prosecution in non-cooperating jurisdictions. The Aurora City Council delayed a 10-year police-ICE cooperation MOU by a 10-1 vote on April 7. The next vote could come any week.

## South Carolina: 3 to 37 in One Year

Fourteen visitors searched South Carolina in the first day — more than any state except the homepage. They found thin coverage. That's been corrected.

South Carolina went from 3 to 37 law enforcement agencies with 287(g) agreements in twelve months. SLED — the state law enforcement division — signed a Task Force Model agreement authorizing 47 agents with street-level immigration enforcement power. Small-town police departments in Gaston (population 800) and Pelion now have deputies who are federal immigration officers.

The state House passed H.4764 on April 2, voting 85-30 to **require every law enforcement agency operating a jail to join 287(g)**. At the subcommittee hearing, every public testifier opposed the bill. No member of the public spoke in favor.

In Lexington County, 12 ICE-dedicated beds are "churning" constantly within 72-hour hold windows. ICE cannot place new detainees until others are moved out. This is the capacity pressure that precedes facility expansion. DHS reimburses participating agencies with a $1,000 quarterly bonus per officer achieving "90-100% assistance to ICE's mission."

3,000 people were arrested in South Carolina in 2025 — double the prior year.

**Update, April 13:** South Carolina remained the most-visited state page through the first day. Nineteen visitors. Seven went to Lexington County specifically. Three studied Charleston with 90% scroll depth.

What we found: Charleston County produced **881 ICE arrests** in nine months — roughly 30% of all South Carolina immigration arrests from a single county. In November 2025, South Carolina Highway Patrol troopers in North Charleston appeared to pull over exclusively Latino-appearing drivers as pretext while ICE agents followed in unmarked vehicles.

In Richland County, a **secret hold room** operates from the Strom Thurmond Federal Building — has since 2014. City leaders said they had no idea. 416 people detained in 2025, up 150% from the Biden era. A June 2025 ICE memo extended the hold time from 12 to 72 hours. The building is now slated for sale by GSA, creating uncertainty about whether the hold room continues.

South Carolina has built a full-stack enforcement infrastructure in the Midlands: street-level enforcement in Charleston, jail processing in Lexington, secret detention and a new $4.3 million OPLA office in Richland. The visitors knew before we did.

## Indiana: The Midwest Hub

Internal DHS documents show Indianapolis under consideration for a warehouse conversion holding 7,000 to 10,000 detainees, targeted for activation by November 2026. Marion County Jail hit capacity and stopped holding for ICE beyond 48 hours. The Miami Correctional Facility in Bunker Hill already activated with 1,000 ICE beds in October 2025.

Three layers of detention infrastructure assembling in a single metro: county jail overflow, state prison conversion, and a proposed warehouse mega-center.

## Oklahoma: The $175 Million Enforcement Economy

Two warehouse proposals were blocked within weeks of each other. In Oklahoma City, property owners withdrew from a 416,000-square-foot warehouse deal after community protests. In Durant, the Choctaw Nation purchased a 1.24-million-square-foot former Big Lots warehouse that ICE had targeted — a sovereign tribal nation directly blocking federal detention infrastructure.

Behind the resistance: an enforcement economy estimated at $175 million annually flowing into Oklahoma law enforcement through federal reimbursements, detention contracts, and performance bonuses. More than 730 Highway Patrol troopers hold 287(g) agreements. At the Tulsa County Jail, 80% of ICE detainees had no prior convictions.

## Oregon: Sanctuary Since 1987, 1,100 Arrests Anyway

Oregon has been a sanctuary state since 1987. Zero 287(g) agreements. Zero overnight detention facilities. Governor Kotek signed eight protection bills on April 9. Voters reaffirmed the sanctuary law in 2018.

ICE arrested 1,100 people in Oregon in 2025 — 75% in the last three months. Operation Black Rose set a target of 30 arrests per day using Palantir's ELITE targeting app. ICE officers confirmed under oath that Palantir identified arrest locations. Only 32% of those arrested had criminal convictions.

In Lane County — where a visitor spent a full minute on the county page — the ACLU alleges the sheriff's office called ICE when people posted bail and gave agents back-door access to the jail. A federal judge ruled in February 2026 that ICE's warrantless arrests violated the Fourth and Fifth Amendments. In Newport, 800 people packed a city council meeting in a town of 10,000 after ICE tried to repurpose a Coast Guard air facility as a holding center.

34 of 36 Oregon sheriffs signed a letter seeking legal cover to cooperate with ICE despite state law.

**Update, April 13:** A visitor spent 2 minutes and 43 seconds on Coos County. Another spent 2 minutes and 12 seconds on Douglas County. Two more visited Jackson County. They were browsing southern Oregon's conservative rural corridor — and they were right that we'd missed something major.

Southern Oregon has developed three distinct mechanisms to make the sanctuary law functionally hollow:

**Surveillance sharing.** Flock automated license plate reader cameras deployed across Medford, Central Point, Klamath Falls, and Josephine County. From 2021 to 2024, the "Southern Oregon Analyst Group" — an informal network of crime analysts from Jackson, Josephine, Douglas, and Klamath counties — shared intelligence directly with ICE and Homeland Security Investigations. Medford PD analysts performed plate lookups for federal agents without warrants. 313 pages of public records document the arrangement.

**Pretext operations.** On July 30, 2025, DEA-led cannabis raids in Jackson County became the cover for 17 ICE arrests. A GEO Group transport bus was pre-staged at the Medford ICE field office before the raids began. Jackson County Sheriff Sickler denied knowing ICE was involved, but his deputies were listed as "primary" at seven of ten raid locations. Workers were transported to Tacoma.

**Property laundering.** ICE's Medford field office sits in a Jackson County-owned building leased through a private intermediary at $0.28 per square foot on a 25-year lease. In March 2026, ICE posted a solicitation for office space in Roseburg (Douglas County) for 300+ employees. These arrangements may not trigger the Sanctuary Promise Act's restrictions on local government cooperation.

In Coos County, ICE scouted three sites for a detention facility after Newport rejected them — the shuttered Shutter Creek prison, federal land in Lakeside, and a third floor of the county jail. The commission voted 2-1 against cooperation, but one commissioner personally courted DHS. The contractor said "moot for now" but left the door open.

The visitor's browsing pattern — Coos longest, Douglas second, Jackson third, Klamath shortest — mapped almost exactly to where the active proposals and fights are. They were checking our coverage against what they already knew on the ground.

## Virginia: The Fastest Reversal

CoreCivic acquired the Farmville Detention Center from Immigration Centers of America on July 1, 2025, for $71.4 million — locking corporate detention into Prince Edward County through at least 2029. Population: 712 of 732 beds.

Under Governor Youngkin, Virginia went from zero 287(g) agreements to 28 in one year. Under Governor Spanberger, inaugurated January 2026, the state rescinded its agreements on day one. The General Assembly passed bills requiring judicial warrants for ICE cooperation, restricting the administrative warrants ICE normally uses.

But the Farmville IGSA runs through the county, not the state. Spanberger's orders don't reach it.

In Buckingham County — rural, adjacent to Farmville, dependent on two state prisons — school resource officers were nominated to serve as immigration enforcement. The Charlottesville school board affirmed a stance against ICE cooperation in April 2026, drawing a sharp contrast with the surrounding counties.

## New Mexico: The Last Holdout

Curry County signed the only 287(g) agreement in New Mexico. Then the legislature passed the Immigrant Safety Act, banning local ICE detention contracts and 287(g) agreements statewide, effective May 20, 2026. The sheriff publicly decried the law. The legislature considered a $10.5 million compensation bill for counties losing detention revenue — a number that reveals the dependency.

## Arkansas: The Mandatory 287(g) Template

Arkansas passed **Act 654** in April 2025 — the "Defense Against Criminal Illegals Act" — requiring every county sheriff in the state to apply for a 287(g) partnership with ICE. Forty agencies have signed. It is the first mandatory 287(g) law in the country, and other states are watching.

The result is visible in Benton County, which produced 450 ICE arrests in nine months — accounting for **4% of all 287(g) arrests in the entire country** from a single county jail. The NW Arkansas pipeline runs from Benton County (arrest and screening) through Washington County (transfer hold in Fayetteville) to Louisiana detention facilities (deportation). One of the most efficient arrest-to-deportation corridors in America.

Then there's the Franklin County prison site. Governor Sanders purchased 815 acres for a planned 3,000-bed, $825 million CoreCivic-operated state prison. On September 22, 2025, the Franklin County chief deputy encountered three ICE agents from the New Orleans office at the site entrance. A special adviser to the governor — the former secretary of the Department of Corrections — arrived minutes later and entered the property with them. An ICE agent said: "We can have this up very quickly, we'll put a temporary one up first."

CoreCivic's retired CEO contributed maximum allowable amounts to the governor's campaigns. The legislature has refused to fund the prison. The local sheriff calls it "fiscally irresponsible." The community is organizing. At 3,000 beds, it would be among the largest ICE detention facilities in existence.

In Springdale — population 89,000, home to the largest Marshallese community outside the Marshall Islands and to Tyson Foods headquarters — Police Chief Wright refused formal ICE cooperation. But Act 654 forced compliance even where local officials resist.

## Idaho: The Sunday at the Racetrack

On October 19, 2025, more than 200 officers — ICE, FBI, Idaho State Police, Canyon County Sheriff, and local departments — raided La Catedral Arena, a horse racing track in Wilder, Canyon County. It was a popular Sunday family gathering for Latino families. The search warrants named five targets in a gambling investigation. Officers detained approximately 400 people — including U.S. citizens and children — and arrested 105 for immigration violations.

Flash-bang grenades. Rubber bullets fired into occupied vehicles. Parents and children zip-tied at gunpoint. A named plaintiff, Juana Rodriguez — a U.S. citizen born in Idaho — was detained with her three-year-old son, also a citizen. Detainees were sorted by "perceived immigration status" based on skin color. Officers denied bathroom access for hours.

The ACLU filed *Rodriguez v. Porter* as a class action on February 10, 2026. Federal Judge Winmill had already ordered 16 detainees released for due process violations. The legal theory: federal agents weaponized local cooperation to conduct a racially targeted immigration sweep under cover of a criminal warrant.

Idaho's ICE arrests rose 797% in the first half of 2025. Ten agencies now hold 287(g) agreements, up from two. The state legislature tried three times to mandate 287(g) for all law enforcement — killed each time by sheriffs citing unfunded mandates. 90% of Idaho's dairy workers are foreign-born. Mass deportation would cause a 45% drop in dairy output and eliminate 56,000 jobs.

## Nevada: The Live Supreme Court Case

On April 9, 2026 — four days before this update — the ACLU filed an appeal to the Nevada Supreme Court challenging Las Vegas Metropolitan Police Department's 287(g) agreement. If the court rules that state law AB376 prohibits 287(g), it could void all four Nevada agreements and disrupt the jail-pipeline model that produces 70% of the state's ICE arrests.

Nevada's arrests tripled from 634 to 2,155 in a single year. Governor Lombardo made Nevada the first state removed from the Trump administration's "sanctuary" list. 70% of arrests happen inside county jails — not community raids. The pipeline runs through the Clark County Detention Center, where LVMPD's 287(g) produced 633 ICE arrests through October 2025.

In Nye County, the cautionary tale. The county jail held ICE detainees under an IGSA for years — and lost money on every bed. A failed ICE audit found no potable water, blocked phone access, and medical neglect. The county was subsidizing ICE by $4 million a year from its general fund. The commission voted 5-0 to terminate.

But CoreCivic's Nevada Southern Detention Center, also in Pahrump, runs at 462 ICE detainees against a 250-bed contract — 84% over capacity. ICE has proposed 450 tent beds that would double Nevada's detention capacity.

## Wyoming: The Counter-Strategy

Wyoming tried to build a dedicated ICE facility once before. From 2017 to 2020, the WyoSayNo campaign defeated a proposed 1,000-bed CoreCivic center in Uinta County. So ICE found another way in.

Instead of one facility that could be fought, ICE distributed detention across county jails — Laramie, Natrona, Sweetwater, Uinta. Lower visibility. Same result. The Uinta County jail that defeated CoreCivic now holds 25 ICE beds at $120 a day. Laramie County swore in 25 deputies for 287(g) and invoiced $75,000 in five months. Sweetwater projects $3 million annually.

Then the state went further. The Wyoming Highway Patrol signed a statewide 287(g) Task Force agreement in July 2025. Any traffic stop, anywhere in Wyoming, can now become an immigration enforcement action. Natrona County received 44 detainees in a single batch from Aurora, Colorado — ICE testified it was sending people with "inactive" cases there to free space. De facto indefinite detention in remote jails, far from courts and attorneys.

CPR and WyoFile analyzed the ICE data: most people arrested in Wyoming had no criminal convictions. The most common charge was DUI.

## Sacramento: The Congresswoman Who Was Blocked at the Door

Sacramento County scored 85 on the heatmap — six IGSAs, three ANC contracts, a facility signal. Four visitors came in the first day. What they found:

The **John Moss Federal Building** at 650 Capitol Mall is being used as an improvised ICE detention facility. Detainees sleeping on bare floors. One water bottle per day. No showers. Cockroaches and rats. Congresswoman Doris Matsui was **physically blocked from entering** — twice — for oversight inspections. When she finally secured a scheduled visit, she called what she saw "sanitized."

On July 17, 2025, a Border Patrol unit detained 12 people in a Florin Road Home Depot parking lot, including a U.S. citizen who was released with a black eye. The ACLU and United Farm Workers filed a motion alleging violation of a court order against racial profiling.

ICE arrests of Asian Americans in the Sacramento area tripled between 2024 and 2025. Low-visibility raids at grocery stores, salons, and restaurants target the region's 19% AAPI population — a pattern that has received almost no national attention.

A federal judge issued an injunction on December 24, 2025, against courthouse arrests in Sacramento — a ruling that could go national.
