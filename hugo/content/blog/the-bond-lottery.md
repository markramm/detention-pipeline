Picture two people with identical stories. Each has lived in the United States for more than a decade. Each was picked up by ICE in the interior — not at the border — and neither has a serious criminal record. One is detained in Cincinnati's federal circuit; the other in New Orleans's.

The first is entitled to a bond hearing, where an immigration judge can decide whether she's a flight risk or a danger and, if not, let her fight her case from home. The second can be held indefinitely, with no hearing at all, until her case resolves or she gives up and accepts deportation.

Same facts. Opposite outcomes. The only thing that changed is the map.

That is the actual state of American immigration-detention law in the summer of 2026 — and ICE has noticed. Increasingly, the agency isn't just detaining people. It's **moving them across the map on purpose**, to land them in the circuits where the door is shut.

## How we got here

For roughly 29 years — across administrations of both parties — noncitizens arrested inside the country and placed in removal proceedings were treated as eligible for a bond hearing under one provision of immigration law (§236(a) of the Immigration and Nationality Act, also cited as 8 U.S.C. §1226(a)).

In September 2025, the Board of Immigration Appeals reinterpreted the statute. It declared that those same people should instead be treated as "applicants for admission" under a *different* provision (§235(b), or §1225(b)) — the one written for people stopped at the border seeking entry, where detention is mandatory and there is no right to a hearing. Overnight, hundreds of thousands of long-settled residents were reclassified into a regime that allows indefinite lockup without review.

Then the lawsuits came, and the federal courts of appeals began to split.

## The split, in order

As of early June 2026, five circuits have ruled, and they've divided **three to two**:

**Against the policy — bond hearings restored:**

- **Fifth Circuit** was first, but on the government's side — *Buenrostro-Mendez v. Bondi* (Feb. 6, 2026, 2-1), written by Judge Edith Jones. It **upheld** indefinite no-bond detention for anyone who entered without inspection, no matter how long ago. It binds Texas, Louisiana, and Mississippi — the circuit holding the largest detained population in the country, with an estimated 15,000 people affected.
- **Eighth Circuit** followed suit — *Herrera Avila v. Bondi* (Mar. 25, 2026, 2-1), which overturned a Minnesota district court's grant of habeas relief. It binds Minnesota, Iowa, Missouri, Arkansas, Nebraska, North Dakota, and South Dakota.

**For the policy — no-bond detention upheld:**

- **Second Circuit** broke the other way — *Cunha v. Freden* (Apr. 28, 2026, 3-0), written by a Trump appointee, Judge Joseph Bianco, who warned the government's reading would create "the broadest mass-detention-without-bond mandate in American history." It binds New York, Connecticut, and Vermont.
- **Eleventh Circuit** joined it — *Hernández Álvarez v. Warden, FDC Miami* (May 6, 2026, 2-1) — holding that interior arrestees get a hearing under §1226(a). It binds Florida, Georgia, and Alabama.
- **Sixth Circuit** made it three — *Lopez-Campos v. Raycraft* (May 11, 2026, 2-1), noting the government had broken a "previously unbroken 29-year streak." Within its territory — Kentucky, Tennessee, Michigan, Ohio — district courts started granting relief immediately; the Western District of Kentucky has been granting roughly three of every four petitions.

Three more circuits have heard arguments and not yet ruled: the **First** (New England, including the Wyatt facility in Rhode Island that has drawn 130-plus habeas petitions), the **Seventh** (Illinois, Indiana, Wisconsin), and the **Ninth** (California, Oregon, Washington) — which has so far leaned the government's way procedurally, staying a pro-bond district ruling and confining it to the Central District of California.

A split this deep, on a question this consequential, almost always ends up at the Supreme Court. Until it does, the map governs.

## The part that turns a map into a strategy

Here's the move that makes this more than a law-school puzzle. Because the right to a hearing attaches to **where you are detained**, ICE can defeat it simply by moving you.

That isn't hypothetical. In Tennessee — inside the Sixth Circuit, where the right now exists — detainees have been routed out of state into Louisiana, which sits in the Fifth Circuit, where it doesn't. The transfer itself strips the very hearing a local judge would have ordered. The same pattern shows up in the project's research again and again: people moved *before* accountability can attach — detainees deported mid-investigation, strike leaders transferred to other facilities, dockets quietly relocated.

Nevada is where a court finally named the tactic for what it is. In *Jacobo-Ramirez v. Noem*, U.S. District Judge Richard Boulware **vacated** the mandatory-detention policy within Nevada in late March 2026, certified a class, and restored bond hearings. Then, in April, when the government tried to move people out of his reach, he issued an emergency order **blocking the transfer of 25 class members out of the state** — treating the transfer itself as the violation, not just a logistics decision.

That is the crux. When a constitutional right depends on geography, and one party controls where people are physically held, the right becomes something that party can switch off by booking a flight.

## Why it matters

For the people inside, this is the difference between fighting a case from home and disappearing into indefinite detention hundreds of miles from family and counsel. For everyone watching the system, it's a warning about how a right erodes — not by being struck down, but by being made contingent on an accident of geography that the enforcing agency can manufacture.

There's a local dimension, too, and it's why this project tracks detention county by county: it now matters enormously *where* a county jail sends the people it hands to ICE. A detainee booked in a pro-bond circuit and bused to a no-bond one has, in practical terms, been moved out of the Constitution's reach. The bond-hearing dockets in the pro-bond districts — and the transfer records that show who gets moved, and when — are among the most important things to watch in the months before the Supreme Court takes this up.

The next domino is the First Circuit, whose ruling will govern New England and the Wyatt detention hub. After that, in all likelihood, the highest court in the country decides whether a bond hearing is a right — or a lottery ticket that depends on which side of a circuit line you happen to be standing on when ICE puts you in a van.
