## The hook

Same person — a longtime resident, arrested in the interior, no serious record.
In Cincinnati's circuit she gets a bond hearing and may go home to fight her case.
In New Orleans's circuit she can be held indefinitely with no hearing at all. The
only variable that changed is the map. That is the state of immigration detention
law in mid-2026, and ICE has started **moving people across that map on purpose.**

## The spine (what happened, in order)

1. **Sept 2025 — the BIA repeal.** The Board of Immigration Appeals reinterpreted
   the law to treat interior-arrested noncitizens as "applicants for admission"
   subject to mandatory detention — ending a ~29-year practice of bond hearings.
2. **The circuits split 3–2 (chronological):**
   - **5th Cir. — *Buenrostro-Mendez v. Bondi*, 166 F.4th 494 (Feb 6, 2026), 2-1.**
     Upheld no-bond detention. Binds TX/LA/MS — the largest detained population
     (~15,000 affected). The outlier that opened the split.
   - **8th Cir. — *Herrera Avila v. Bondi*, No. 25-3248 (Mar 25, 2026), 2-1.**
     Same result; overturned a Minnesota district habeas grant. Binds MN/IA/MO/AR/
     NE/SD/ND.
   - **2nd Cir. — *Cunha v. Freden* (Apr 28, 2026), 3-0.** FIRST to reject the
     policy — Trump-appointed judge wrote it. Binds NY/CT/VT.
   - **11th Cir. — *Hernández Álvarez v. Warden, FDC Miami*, No. 25-14065
     (May 6, 2026), 2-1.** Bond hearings under §1226(a), not mandatory detention
     under §1225(b). Binds FL/GA/AL.
   - **6th Cir. — *Lopez-Campos v. Raycraft* (May 11, 2026), 2-1.** Third to block;
     district courts granting relief immediately (W.D. Ky. ~75% grant rate).
     Binds KY/TN/MI/OH.
   - **Pending (argued, no ruling):** 1st (MA/NH/ME/RI), 7th (IL/IN/WI),
     9th (WA/OR/CA — stayed a pro-bond district order).
3. **The legal hinge:** §1225(b)/§235(b) ("applicants for admission," mandatory)
   vs. §1226(a)/§236(a) (interior arrests, bond eligible). Every pro-bond ruling
   turns on the same point — people already living here aren't "seeking admission."

## The investigative angle (this is what makes it a piece, not a summary)

**Transfers as forum-shopping — the venue lottery is being gamed.** Because the
right attaches to *where you're detained*, ICE can defeat it by moving you:
- **TN → LA:** detainees picked up in the 6th Circuit (bond) bused to Louisiana
  (5th Circuit, no bond) — documented in the Tennessee research; strips the very
  hearing the local court would grant. (See `davidson-county-tn-*`.)
- **NV pushback:** in *Jacobo-Ramirez v. Noem*, a Nevada judge **blocked** the
  out-of-state transfer of 25 class members and restored bond hearings — the
  first court to treat transfer itself as the rights violation.
- The pattern rhymes with other "move them before accountability lands" tactics
  this project has tracked (detainees deported mid-PREA-investigation in MI;
  strike leaders transferred in NJ).

## Why it matters (the close)

- It turns a constitutional question into a geographic accident — and a logistics
  decision ICE controls.
- It's almost certainly **SCOTUS-bound**; until then the map governs. The 1st
  Circuit ruling (New England, governs the Wyatt RI hub + 130+ habeas cases) is
  the next domino to watch.
- Local angle for our readers: it raises the stakes of *where* a county sends its
  detainees, and of the bond-hearing habeas dockets in pro-bond districts.
