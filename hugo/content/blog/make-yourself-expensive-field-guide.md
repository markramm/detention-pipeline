> **You don't have to make the surveillance machine blind. You just have to make it expensive.**

Surveillance is a **volume business**. Data brokers — and the government buyers downstream of them — run on cheap, accurate, high-volume personal data. Every record you remove raises their supply cost. Every inaccuracy you introduce degrades their product. Every non-consent you assert builds the legal record. You can't opt out of the system alone. But you can make yourself a more expensive target, and at scale that squeezes the market's margin.

**Read this before you spend a dollar:** the **free DIY route beats every paid service.** A 2024 Consumer Reports / Tall Poppy study (32 volunteers, 7 paid services, 13 people-search sites, four-month window) found the paid services removed only about **35%** of records — while **doing it manually hit about 70%**, beating every paid service tested. The paid services sell you something that is free and worse. Don't pay the opt-out tax.

---

## Pick your level

- **Most people → Tier A.**
- **Activists, organizers, immigrants, journalists, ICE-watch volunteers → Tier A *and* Tier B.**

---

## Tier A — everyone should do this

### 1. Reduce your data-broker exposure (this is move #1)

Broker data is what government surveillance is *built on*, so this is the highest-leverage thing you can do. Pick the tool that fits you — all of these are free or open-source:

| If you are… | Use | What it does | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| A general / phone user | **Permission Slip** (by Consumer Reports) | Polished iOS/Android app: swipe to send "do not sell" + deletion requests across hundreds of companies | **Free** (skip the $60/yr "Plus" tier) |
| Comfortable with a web app | **Eraser** (open-source) | Sends GDPR/CCPA removal requests to **750+ brokers**; flags which need manual follow-up | **Free / open-source** |
| Higher-risk, want maximum privacy | **data-broker-removal** (open-source) | Runs **fully offline** — your data never leaves your machine; generates checklists + email templates | **Free / open-source** |
| Want to do it by hand | **Yael Grauer's "Big Ass Data Broker Opt-Out List"** | The canonical, maintained list. Work it yourself, ~70% effective | **Free** |

Also free and open-source: **PrivacyBot**, **YourDigitalRights.org**.

### 2. Use Signal for messaging

Protects the *content* of your messages. (It does not make you anonymous.)

### 3. Move email to Proton or Tuta, off Gmail

These protect content — **not your identity**. As Freedom of the Press Foundation puts it, assume you are identifiable when using Proton. It raises the baseline; it is not a cloak.

### 4. Use Firefox (hardened) as your browser

For most people. Brave works too, with eyes open.

### 5. Search with DuckDuckGo, Startpage, or Brave Search

Anything but Google as your default.

### 6. Don't oversell a VPN to yourself

VPNs are mostly oversold — they do **not** make you anonymous. If you use one, the honest, audited, open-source shortlist is **Mullvad, IVPN, or Mozilla VPN.**

## Tier B — higher-risk: everything above, plus

- **Anonymity is not the same as privacy.** Signal, Proton, and a VPN raise your baseline but do not make you anonymous. For anonymity, use **Tor Browser** (or Mullvad Browser), and assume email is identifiable.
- **Compartmentalize.** Separate identities, devices, and accounts for organizing versus personal life. Burner numbers. Use Signal usernames instead of your phone number where you can.
- **Device discipline.** Full-disk encryption. Strong **passcodes — not just biometrics** (a fingerprint or face can be compelled more easily at a border or an arrest). Minimize what's on the phone you carry to an action.
- **The ambient layer** — doorbell cameras, automated license-plate readers — is largely outside any individual setting. The realistic move is **collective and political**: local ALPR ordinances, pushing agencies off Flock and Ring data-sharing.
- **The canonical guide for this tier is the EFF's Surveillance Self-Defense** (ssd.eff.org). Point people there rather than improvising.

## Why "do not sell" is more than a gesture

Telling brokers "do not sell my data" doesn't, by itself, create a Fourth Amendment right — but it's not symbolic either. The Supreme Court's *Carpenter* decision (2018) already moved the line on what counts as a "reasonable expectation of privacy." Mass, documented non-consent is how that line gets renegotiated, and it attacks the weak point in the doctrine brokers rely on: the claim that you handed your data over *voluntarily*.

There's already proof this works. **Global Privacy Control** started as a voluntary browser signal that said "do not sell." Then California's privacy law made honoring it *legally required* — and the state's very first enforcement action ($1.2 million from Sephora, 2022) was for ignoring it. Symbolic to enforceable in a few years. You're not claiming the right. You're doing the thing that creates it.

---

## The bigger fix is collective

The individual moves above matter, but the real leverage is above the individual. The **Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act** — which would close the loophole that lets the government simply *buy* broker data instead of getting a warrant — passed the House in 2024 but stalled in the Senate. It is not law.

The highest-leverage thing isn't a tool at all. Because doing it yourself beats the paid services, the scarce resource is **someone sitting with you for an hour.** Libraries, mutual-aid groups, and sanctuary congregations can run "data-detox" clinics. The EFF runs an annual **Opt-Out October.** Building that capability lowers the tax for everyone; the legislation would abolish it.
